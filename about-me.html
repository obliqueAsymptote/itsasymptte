Who is she?

If I had to introduce myself in as many adjectives in one sentence as possible to grab your attention, I'm an 18 year-old, hard-working and ambitious, esteemed and educated girl from the US. I have an undying obsession with computer science and math, striving to teach myself something new everyday and push myself beyond my limits. I never like to settle and I want to exceed my peers by a far mile, and I'd like to think that this drive of mine has gotten me a lot of places that I wouldn't have gotten otherwise. This isn't me being cocky, this is me genuinely acknowledging that my capacity to learn is directly under my control so like any rational person, it must be taken advantage of.

How did it all start?

I've always been into computer from a young age. I used to try to constantly bypass controls my parents would put on me which familiarized me with powershell and vpns and unbeknownst to them, sparked a drive in me.
I then self taught myself basic front-end web dev at 12 very poorly and never went anywhere with it because I was too busy writing fanfictions about my favorite animes LOL
Then once I hit my teenage years, my parents thought it was a good idea to take me to europe to study abroad for the duration of my life before adulthood, as I was excelling beyond what the US education system could challenge me on. It was there that I did computer science at GCSE and A-level, reaching A's across each assessment and coursework.
I had familiarized myself with python at late 13/early 14 and didn't take it seriously until I reached 16, when I started my A-level. We would be given class assignments in groups to do simple programs like calculating football tickets, making a simple web scraper (BS4 THE GOAT)!!!
That's when I realized I couldn't excel as long as the idea of APIs and JSON parsing was foreign to me. That sparked this absolute lock in which made me go from a novice in python to not only knowing it now innately with advanced paradigms, but also picking up C++, daily driving linux, and even teaching myself college level maths to better understand algorithms. Genuinely within a little over a year, I had exceeded people I used to look up to.

Near the end of my A-levels, we had mandated coursework worth 20% of our full grade over the 2 years. I took the opportunity to push myself as I thought I'd never get the chance to give myself a straight few months to work on something. This started my rabbithole of AI, specifically deep learning. While others were struggling on whether to do a measly quiz app with simple SQL queries or a weather app using the same weather API every new CS student uses, I was adamant on doing a facial recognition control system.
However an issue came to light: I knew absolutely zilch about how AI worked. Now, given I am an incredibly adaptable person, I persisted. I taught myself linear algebra and calculus so I could get the hang of matrices for datasets, and backpropagation to better work with my model. I would stay up watching 3blue1brown videos to better understand how neural networks worked, I differentiated between supervised, unsupervised, reinforcement learning, I delved into recurrent vs. convolutional networks- It felt like I was destined to be in this subgenre of CS. The math, combined with the control I was given, this obsessive drive skyrocketed.
Now granted I was only given about 4 months to do this project and I was balancing other subjects and studying to death to rightfully gain one of the top students in the whole college (which I earned a plaque for :3), I had to abstract my project a bit. I didn't use raw libraries to train models myself, however I had to store encodings myself and give my program a threshold of a percentage match up of a face stored in the SQL database that was decent enough. Additionally, I had to use numpy to reduce false positives that were being picked up. Everytime I resented myself for not being the reincarnation of terry davis himself, I'd mediate myself by saying at least I'm getting my foot in the door.
Well, I certaintly did; I got the hightest in my class and I had a working face recognition access system. I continued with my studies and worked hard to understand the theory: Logic circuits, different network devices, boolean algebra, addressing modes and reference vs value parameter passing. I had consumed so much knowledge that when it came to exam time where I'd witness people have uncontrollable breakdowns, I'd take the exam for the whole 2 hours and a half smiling, seeing it as a challenge.

Now let's rewind a bit- since my college was 2 years from 16 to 18, I had met my ex-boyfriend at 17 who opened up the door to cybersecurity. I quickly picked up a lot in that field; Linux cli tools, CVE POCs, opsec, social engineering, and I even went down the rabbithole of telegram groupchats.
Let me preface by saying I was never involved in anything illicit, I mean these kids were pretty harmless. They vibe-coded (god i love that word) a barely working UI and couldn't tell the difference between a 1D and 2D array. When I had met my ex-boyfriend, I was a complete novice at this and programming and by the end of my relationship, I had exceeded him by a far mile and ended my relationship with him.
I'm not here to trauma dump on people who come and go in my life, this is to simply inform on my journey to who I am today and who I will continue to grow to be.

After I finished my A levels in early july of 2025, I couldn't attend university where I was residing as I had better options coming back to the USA, so that is exactly what I did. My mom supported me and we all relocated back to the states and applied for top universities in my state. Funnily enough, I even changed majors. Originally I was going to major in IT but I realized that my brain was more geared for tackling challeneges in software and coming up with efficient methods in solving problems rather than routine IT protocols. And further, as my love for solving math problems in my free time flourished, naturally I gravitated towards a bachelors in computer science. So I spent my whole summer brushing up on any math I'll need during uni and advancing my coding skills to the point where my first 2 years of university content looked like a revision session than learning new content LOL.
